The main event · Startup at Jantar Mantar

The incubator is open. Pick a track.

Six tracks, each aimed at a failure every Indian recognises. Pitch into one, find co-founders in the crowd and online, prototype small, open-source it — and ship tools the system can't ignore.

Track 01 · ExamTech

Leak-proof examination infrastructure

Paper-trail tracking, encrypted question banks, exam-centre auditing apps, anonymous leak-reporting hotlines with evidence vaults.

Track 02 · JanData

Governance data in people's hands

Dashboards that turn budgets, MGNREGA rolls and school data into plain Hindi/regional-language scorecards every citizen can read.

Track 03 · RTI Tools

Right to Information, minus the friction

One-tap RTI filing, template libraries, deadline trackers and public archives of replies — transparency as an app, not an ordeal.

Track 04 · Ward-Up

Bottom-up local governance

Ward-level issue reporting, panchayat meeting trackers, participatory budgeting pilots — democracy rebuilt street by street.

Track 05 · YuvaJobs

Honest pathways to work

Verified vacancy boards, exam-calendar sanity tools, skill-exchange collectives — because rage at unemployment deserves an outlet that pays.

Track 06 · SatyaCheck

Truth infrastructure

Community fact-checking of sarkari claims, promise-trackers for manifestos, archives that remember what politicians said.

The movement behind the incubator

An insult became an identity.

In May 2026, when activists and unemployed youth were dismissed from the highest quarters as "cockroaches" and "parasites of society," young India did something unexpected: it wore the insult as armour.

Within a week, the satirical Cockroach Janta Party — a wordplay every Indian instantly understood — had lakhs of sign-ups and tens of millions of followers. What began as memes turned into marches: exam paper leaks, cancelled tests, delayed results and vanishing jobs had given a generation more than enough reason.

Since 20 June, supporters have held a continuous presence at Jantar Mantar — protesting peacefully with the national flag in one hand and a book in the other, symbolising the right to education and equal opportunity. Volunteers arrive daily with food and water. Veterans of public conscience have joined; a hunger strike stands alongside the students.

That movement is the wind at this incubator's back — the crowd, the urgency and the audience. The building comes first here; the story is why it matters.

What the movement demands

Not revolution. Just fairness.

The demands on the street are simple enough to fit on a placard — and reasonable enough that refusing them is the real scandal.

01

Accountability at the top

Resignation of the Union Education Minister over the NEET 2026 paper leak and repeated examination failures on his watch.

02

Leak-proof examinations

End-to-end reform of how papers are set, printed, transported and conducted — so that one leak cannot destroy ten lakh futures.

03

Results on time, every time

Fixed statutory timelines for results and counselling. A student's year should never disappear into administrative silence.

04

Transparent testing agencies

Independent audits, published error reports and real consequences for the agencies that run national examinations.

05

Justice for the affected

Fair remedies for students harmed by leaks, cancellations and marking irregularities — including those driven to despair.

06

A seat for the young

Structured youth consultation in education policy — because the people who take the exams deserve a voice in how they're run.
